Neglected tropical diseases and vitamin B12: a review of the current evidence

Layden, A. J. and Tase, K. and Finkelstein, J. L.

Transactions of the Royal Society of Tropical Medicine and Hygiene (2018) 112: 423–435

DOI: 10.1093/trstmh/try078

Abstract

Vitamin B_\textrm12 deficiency is an urgent public health problem that disproportionately affects individuals in low- and middle-income settings, where the burden of neglected tropical diseases (NTDs) is also unacceptably high. Emerging evidence supports a potential role of micronutrients in modulating the risk and severity of NTDs. However, the role of vitamin B_\textrm12 in NTD pathogenesis is unknown. This systematic review was conducted to evaluate the evidence on the role of vitamin B_\textrm12 in the etiology of NTDs. Ten studies were included in this review: one study using an in vitro/animal model, eight observational human studies and one ancillary analysis conducted within an intervention trial. Most research to date has focused on vitamin B_\textrm12 status and helminthic infections. One study examined the effects of vitamin B_\textrm12 interventions in NTDs in animal and in vitro models. Few prospective studies have been conducted to date to examine the role of vitamin B_\textrm12 in NTDs. The limited literature in this area constrains our ability to make specific recommendations. Larger prospective human studies are needed to elucidate the role of vitamin B_\textrm12 in NTD risk and severity in order to inform interventions in at-risk populations.
